I'm not sure this article offered a huge deal extra over the author's first part, but I did find some of his analysis interesting.

Some parts that were particularly of note as part of the discussion of some of the others here (please correct and forgive me if I misrepresent anyone's perspective).

He discusses how the modern process itself is Islam's 'other', and how it was 'signified as such that it behaves in the exact opposite manner as Islam'. I believe this is actually in accordance to Mohammed's point about needing to return to the core principles of ihsan and taqwa that we should be imbuing into our lives and our communities, as the situation we are in is partially due to this ideology that has shaped the modern world being the polar opposite to Islam. To counteract something you must practise its opposite. 

A need and requirement for balance and treading of the middle path is also needed however, just as Allah has said He will not change a condition of a people until they change themselves, we must first and foremost improve our inner states, but this doesn't necessarily mean we must shun the outer states. The conditions and requirements needed for our communities to rise up ultimately depend on the communities themselves to take onus, and producing those capable of effectuating the change. The author's project seems to be more aligned with just equipping Muslims to more efficiently and wisely attempt to navigate the future, rather than fall into the same failure and misunderstandings of past in thinking something like a particular political party will be our salvation. 

The author of the article closes by saying that the way out of our predicament is to '*create communities of experts, who can rise above our weakened state of being*'. Again, I don't think this is dichotomous to the return to the inward. The solution absolutely is to hold firm to the commandments of Allah, but this isn't mutually exclusive (not that it was claimed to be) with attempting to gain an understanding of the world for our own betterment. 

On one hand we can have absolute Luddite retreat and on the other hand the idea that all our problems will be solved through socio-political nous. The best of affairs is the moderate. Rectification of the self, is paramount and probably even a pre-requisite, but attempting to navigate the future--for the betterment of the Ummah--is also important. I agree that whatever our harvest is, with Allah's will, it will be prosperous but we also have responsibility of almost tying the camel so to speak.
