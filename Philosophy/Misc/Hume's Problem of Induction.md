David Hume's problem of induction is a critical challenge he raised against the philosophical justification for inductive reasoning and causality. It is considered one of the most influential problems in epistemology and the philosophy of science. Here's an overview of Hume's problem of induction:

1) Inductive reasoning is the process of inferring general laws or conclusions from finite observations of particular instances. For example, observing the sun rise in the east every morning for thousands of days and inductively inferring that "the sun will always rise in the east."

2) Hume argued that no matter how many instances of a phenomenon we observe, it does not logically justify the inference that it will continue that way in the future or that it describes a universal law of nature.

3) He pointed out that our expectation that the future will resemble the past is based on habit, custom or our psychological conditioning from experiencing constant conjunctions of events, not on any logical necessity.

4) There is no rational justification for the inductive leap from the proposition "I have observed A and B conjoined in the past" to the conclusion "Therefore, A and B will always be conjoined in the future."

5) Causality, which is foundational to scientific laws and explanations, cannot be empirically proven or deduced with certainty. We experience constant conjunctions of events, but not necessarily any binding causal connection between them.

6) Hume argued that inductive reasoning concerning matters of fact is never logically conclusive, but is based on habits of mind formed from past experiences. It relies on the assumption of the uniformity of nature, an assumption that itself cannot be rationally proven.

In essence, Hume revealed a fundamental problem - inductive reasoning from finite empirical observations can at best provide provisional evidence, but not apodictic certainty about the existence of natural laws, causality or what the future holds. No number of confirming instances can logically establish the truth of universal generalizations.

This poses a major challenge to empirical science, which relies heavily on inductive methodology. It forced philosophers to grapple with the rational grounds for our ampliative inferences and scientific reasoning. Responses have included Bayesian approaches, Popper's critical rationalism, and other attempts to place induction on firmer logical and evidentiary footings, but Hume's problem remains highly influential.
