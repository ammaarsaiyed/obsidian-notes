### People First: Making People Comfortable Raising Problems

Question:
How would you ensure people feel comfortable coming to you with a problem?

Answer:

> I would make it clear through behaviour, not slogans. I would respond calmly when issues are raised, thank people for raising risks early, and separate the person from the problem. In delivery, I would use regular check-ins, design reviews and open questions so people do not feel they have to wait until something is serious.
>
> A practical example is [ADJUST] when working across teams on a technical issue, I tried to make the problem visible without blaming anyone. I focused the conversation on what we knew, what we did not know, and what support was needed. That helped people contribute honestly and solve the issue faster.

Follow-up:
How would you support others in this role?

> I would support through pairing, clear technical patterns, unblocking decisions, sharing context, and protecting space for people to learn. In a new AI engineering team, confidence matters because the technology is moving quickly and people need room to ask basic questions.

### Bold: Weighing New Ideas Against Cost And Time

Question:
Describe a time you weighed up the benefits of implementing something new with the cost and time.

Answer:

> The LLM deployment framework is a strong example. The new idea was using prefix caching and asynchronous batching to improve GenAI serving performance. The benefit was potentially much better latency and concurrency; the cost was engineering time, added complexity and operational risk.
>
> I approached it by researching the pattern, identifying the performance bottleneck, implementing it in a controlled way, and measuring the impact. The result was a 91% response-time reduction, 86% improvement in concurrent success and support for over 1,000 requests per day.
>
> I explained the decision in terms of business and operational value: better responsiveness, better capacity and a more viable production service. I would also be clear about the complexity introduced and the need for monitoring.

Follow-up:
How did you explain your decision to others?

> I would avoid making it sound like technology for technology's sake. I would show the problem, the options, the trade-offs, the measured impact and the controls needed to run it safely.

### Inclusive: Valuing Diverse Perspectives

Question:
Outline the steps you would take to create an environment where different perspectives are valued.

Answer:

> I would design discussions so the loudest voice does not automatically win. That means sharing context before meetings, inviting written input, asking quieter people directly but respectfully, and making decision criteria explicit. For AI work, diverse perspectives are especially important because different people spot different risks: customer harm, accessibility, operational edge cases, data quality, explainability or colleague workflow concerns.
>
> I would adapt my behaviour by listening first, checking my assumptions, and being willing to change my view when new evidence or lived experience shows a blind spot.

Follow-up:
How would you know it worked?

> I would look for better participation, earlier risk identification, fewer late surprises, and feedback from team members that they feel able to challenge decisions.

### Sustainable: Long-Term Impact Over Short-Term Benefit

Question:
Tell me about a time you prioritised long-term impact over short-term benefit.

Answer:

> In EDP, building Consumer Data Products required more discipline than producing one-off data outputs. It would often be faster to create a local query or analysis, but the long-term value came from trusted, reusable, governed assets with proper transformations, orchestration, access controls and release process.
>
> By using dbt, BigQuery, Cloud Composer, Terraform and CI/CD practices, the work became more reusable and supportable. That long-term thinking is directly relevant to AI MVPs: a quick demo is useful, but reusable patterns and operational controls create lasting value.

Follow-up:
How would you ensure others consider the bigger picture?

> I would make long-term costs visible: support burden, rework, governance gaps, duplicated effort and customer risk. I would also give teams practical templates so doing the sustainable thing is easier.
