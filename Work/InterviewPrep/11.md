### 2. How Do You Assess And Troubleshoot Model Performance Issues, Especially Across Multiple Projects Or Teams?

Detailed answer:

> I start by clarifying what kind of performance has degraded, because "model performance" can mean different things. It might mean predictive quality, such as F1, precision, recall or calibration. It might mean system performance, such as latency, throughput or timeout rate. It might mean business performance, such as reduced adoption or poor operational outcomes.
>
> My first step is triage. I would ask: when did the issue start, who is affected, what changed recently, what is the severity, and do we need immediate mitigation? I would check release history, data pipeline status, model or prompt versions, monitoring dashboards, error logs and user feedback.
>
> Then I split the diagnosis by layer. At the data layer, I check schema changes, missing values, freshness, drift, class balance, label quality and leakage. At the model layer, I check prediction distributions, confusion matrix changes, thresholds, subgroup performance and representative error examples. At the GenAI layer, I check prompt changes, retrieval quality, tool-call success, context length, safety refusals, hallucination rate and output schema failures. At the system layer, I check latency, concurrency, rate limits, dependency failures and resource utilisation.
>
> If multiple projects or teams are involved, the leadership challenge is to create structure. I would establish severity, assign owners, create a shared incident or investigation log, agree communication cadence and avoid everyone looking at the same layer while other layers are ignored. I would prioritise by customer impact, regulatory risk, operational severity and reversibility.
>
> The mitigation depends on the issue. It could be rollback, threshold adjustment, disabling a risky feature, refreshing a data product, falling back to deterministic logic, adding human review or rate-limiting traffic. After the immediate fix, I would add regression tests, monitoring or process changes so the issue is easier to detect next time.

Follow-up pointers:

- If asked about drift: cover input drift, concept drift, label drift, prediction drift and business-process drift.
- If asked about GenAI: include retrieval quality, prompt version, tool failure, context degradation and unsupported claims.
- If asked about multiple teams: emphasise ownership, triage, communication and avoiding duplicated effort.
- Example to cite: 96% F1 model for model-quality troubleshooting; LLM deployment for latency/concurrency; EDP for pipeline/data-quality issues.

### 4. How Do You Ensure Responsible AI Practices And Model Governance Within Your Team?

Detailed answer:

> I treat responsible AI as an engineering requirement, not a final compliance step. I would start by classifying the use case: who is affected, what decision is influenced, what data is used, what happens if the output is wrong, and whether the system is advisory or automated.
>
> Then I map harms to controls. For a complaints or customer-resolution AI system, harms could include incorrect recommendations, missed evidence, unfair outcomes, leakage of sensitive customer data, prompt injection, hallucinated policy interpretation or colleagues over-trusting the output. Controls would include least-privilege access, approved data sources, human review, source citations, structured outputs, schema validation, refusal behaviour, evaluation cases, audit logs and monitoring.
>
> Governance also means data discipline. My EDP experience is relevant because I worked with governed data products, secure views, policy tagging, authorisation and GCP access controls. For AI systems, the same thinking applies to retrieval and tool access: the model should only access what the workflow and user are permitted to access.
>
> For model evaluation, I would define acceptance criteria before release. For traditional ML, that might be F1, precision, recall, calibration and subgroup performance. For GenAI, I would evaluate groundedness, unsupported claims, retrieval relevance, tool-call correctness, output validity, safety and human usefulness. I would include edge cases and adversarial cases, not just happy paths.
>
> Finally, governance has to continue after release. I would set up monitoring, user feedback, periodic review, drift checks, incident routes, rollback criteria and clear ownership. A model or AI system that no one owns after launch is not governed.

Follow-up pointers:

- Responsible AI terms: fairness, safety, explainability, privacy, accountability, reliability.
- Practical controls: evals, access controls, logs, human oversight, monitoring.
- Lloyds angle: customer trust, regulated environment, sensitive data, auditability.
- Lead angle: create team patterns and checklists so governance is repeatable.

## Python Questions

### 5. How Have You Optimised Python Code For Performance In Large-Scale Data Science Projects?

Detailed answer:

> My strongest performance example is the production LLM deployment framework in Colleague Channels. The problem was not just slow Python code in isolation; it was GenAI serving performance under concurrent demand. I researched and implemented prefix caching and asynchronous batching, which reduced response times by 91%, improved concurrent success by 86%, and supported more than 1,000 GenAI requests per day on internal GPU infrastructure.
>
> The way I approach performance is to profile before optimising. I try to identify whether the bottleneck is CPU, I/O, network, database, model inference, serial processing, concurrency, or repeated work. The solution depends on the bottleneck. For data processing, that might mean vectorised operations, better SQL pushdown, avoiding repeated dataframe scans, chunking or batching. For I/O-heavy workflows, async can help. For model-serving workloads, batching, caching and queueing can be more important than micro-optimising individual functions.
>
> Prefix caching helped because repeated prompt prefixes did not need to be recomputed in the same way for every request. Async batching helped because concurrent requests could be handled more efficiently, improving throughput and success under load. I also cared about observability, because performance improvements need to be measured through p50/p95 latency, timeout rate, error rate, throughput and resource utilisation.
>
> The lesson is that performance optimisation should be evidence-led. I do not want to make code clever for its own sake. I want to remove the bottleneck that matters to the user or production system.

Follow-up pointers:

- Async example: LLM serving with asynchronous batching.
- Multi-threading: if asked, explain that threads can help blocking I/O, while async is often cleaner for high-concurrency I/O workflows; CPU-bound work may need multiprocessing or distributed compute.
- Mention profiling: logs, timings, tracing, DynaTrace, p95 latency.
- Mention maintainability: performance changes need tests and monitoring.

### 6. What Strategies Do You Use To Mentor Junior Team Members In Python Best Practices And Debugging?

Detailed answer:

> I would mentor through a mix of pairing, code review, examples and debugging habits. The goal is not only to correct code, but to help someone think like an engineer.
>
> For Python best practice, I would focus on readability, clear function boundaries, type hints where useful, meaningful names, small testable units, error handling, logging, configuration management and avoiding hidden state. In data and AI work, I would also stress reproducibility: deterministic data splits, versioned prompts or configs, stable dependencies and clear experiment tracking.
>
> For debugging, I would teach a structured approach. First reproduce the issue. Then reduce it to the smallest failing case. Inspect inputs and assumptions. Add logging or breakpoints at boundaries. Check recent changes. Separate data issues from code issues. Write a failing test if possible. Then fix the root cause rather than patching the symptom.
>
> I would also use code reviews as a mentoring tool. I would explain why I am asking for a change, not just request it. For example, if a function mixes data access, model invocation and formatting, I would explain how separation makes the code easier to test and debug. In GenAI work, I would review prompts, tool schemas and evals with the same seriousness as code.
>
> As a lead engineer, I would try to make good practice easy by providing templates: project structure, logging examples, evaluation harness examples, token-tracking utilities and checklists for production readiness.

Follow-up pointers:

- If asked how to support different skill levels: pair more with juniors, use design reviews for mid-levels, use decision records for seniors.
- If asked about advanced debugging: talk about tracing, dependency isolation, regression tests, data snapshots and production logs.
- Link to your experience: reusable token-tracking module, presentations, technical direction, code reviews.

### 9. How Do You Balance Innovation With Practicality When Prioritising Foundation Model Deployment?

Detailed answer:

> I balance innovation and practicality by making the decision criteria explicit. Foundation models can produce impressive demos quickly, but deployment should depend on value, risk, feasibility and operational readiness.
>
> First, I ask whether a foundation model is actually needed. If deterministic logic, a traditional ML model or a simpler search workflow solves the problem with lower risk, I would choose that. If the task needs language reasoning, summarisation, retrieval over unstructured documents, tool use or multi-step investigation, then a foundation model may be justified.
>
> Second, I look at risk. What data is being used? Is the output customer-impacting? Does it need human review? Can we explain or trace the answer? What happens if it hallucinates? In a Lloyds context, risk is not separate from delivery. It decides the design.
>
> Third, I look at operational practicality: latency, cost, monitoring, support model, evaluation, integration and ownership. My LLM deployment work is a good example of practicality: prefix caching and async batching made foundation model serving more viable by improving response time and concurrency.
>
> Finally, I would prioritise experiments that reduce uncertainty quickly. If the question is "Can an agent use tools reliably?", test tool-call accuracy. If the question is "Can RAG ground answers in policy?", test retrieval and citation accuracy. If the question is "Can this scale?", test latency, throughput and cost.

Follow-up pointers:

- Use "innovation with controls" as the theme.
- Mention stop criteria: weak evals, unclear ownership, high customer harm, simpler method available.
- Mention decision matrix: value, risk, feasibility, cost, supportability, explainability.

> I start by making sure the problem is framed as a decision, not just a request for a model. I ask what decision needs to be improved, who makes it, what data is available, what action follows from the output, and how success will be measured.
>
> Then I assess the data. I look at provenance, quality, completeness, bias, timeliness, leakage risk, access permissions and whether the available data actually represents the decision context. If labels exist, I check label quality and class balance. If labels do not exist, I consider proxy metrics, SME review, historical outcomes, synthetic examples or unsupervised approaches.
>
> Next, I establish baselines. A simple rule, SQL query or interpretable model may be enough. I would only add complexity if it improves the business metric enough to justify cost, risk and maintenance. In a bank, explainability and governance often matter as much as raw performance.
>
> For evaluation, I choose metrics that reflect the business trade-off. For classification, that may be precision, recall, F1 or calibration. For regression, error metrics and business tolerances matter. For GenAI, I would evaluate groundedness, relevance, unsupported claims, safety, latency and user usefulness.
>
> Finally, I think about adoption. A technically good model fails if stakeholders do not trust it or the workflow cannot use it. I would explain the model in terms of decisions, examples, limitations, monitoring and human oversight.

Follow-up pointers:

- Important modelling considerations: data quality, leakage, baseline, metric, interpretability, operational route.
- Evaluate without labels: SME review, proxy outcomes, human rubrics, synthetic cases, pairwise comparison, delayed labels.
- Choose between models: business metric, risk, interpretability, latency, cost, maintainability.
- Persuade stakeholders: start with their decision, show evidence, pilot safely, explain limitations.

### 24. Batch Versus Streaming: Differences, Patterns And Scaling

Detailed answer:

> Batch processing handles data on a schedule or in chunks. It is suitable for daily data product refreshes, periodic model scoring, reporting or offline evaluation. Streaming handles events continuously and is suitable when the business needs low-latency reaction to new events.
>
> Batch is simpler to reason about, easier to reprocess and often easier to govern. Streaming can provide faster reaction times but adds complexity around ordering, backpressure, replay, state, exactly-once or at-least-once processing, and operational monitoring.
>
> Common patterns include scheduled batch pipelines, micro-batching, event-driven processing, online inference APIs and asynchronous queues. For high demand, I would scale by identifying bottlenecks: data source, orchestration, model endpoint, tool latency, queue depth or downstream system. Techniques include horizontal scaling, batching, caching, partitioning, backpressure, rate limiting and graceful degradation.
>
> For GenAI specifically, I would also consider token cost, context length, model rate limits, batching opportunities, caching repeated prompts, and fallback to simpler models or deterministic flows where appropriate.

Follow-up pointers:

- Use EDP for batch/orchestration with Cloud Composer.
- Use LLM deployment for high-demand serving, caching and async batching.
- Mention that streaming is only justified when latency needs demand it.

### 25. How Do You Monitor System Performance And Drift In Production?

Detailed answer:

> I would monitor four layers: data, model, system and business outcome.
>
> At the data layer, I would monitor schema changes, missingness, freshness, volume, distribution drift and data-quality checks. At the model layer, I would monitor prediction distribution, confidence/calibration, precision/recall/F1 when labels arrive, thresholds and subgroup performance where relevant. At the system layer, I would monitor latency, throughput, error rate, timeout rate, dependency failures and resource utilisation. At the business layer, I would monitor whether the model is improving the workflow: adoption, time saved, complaint outcomes, manual override rates or user feedback.
>
> For GenAI and agentic systems, I would add prompt or model version, retrieval relevance, citation accuracy, unsupported claims, safety flags, tool-call success, output schema validity, token usage, cost and human-review outcomes.
>
> Drift can happen because customer behaviour changes, upstream data pipelines change, business processes change, labels change, external conditions change, or the population using the system changes. In GenAI systems, drift can also come from document changes, prompt changes, model upgrades or tool changes.
>
> Monitoring should lead to action. I would define alert thresholds, investigation steps, rollback criteria, retraining or revalidation triggers and ownership.

### 26. How Would You Evaluate An Agentic AI MVP When There Is No Simple Ground-Truth Label?

>
> Where there is no label, I would use SME review with a clear rubric, historical cases, synthetic edge cases, pairwise comparison between versions and human feedback from pilot users. I would also track operational metrics such as latency, cost, tool failures and escalation rate.
>
> The important point is that "no perfect labels" does not mean "no evaluation". It means designing multiple forms of evidence.

### 27. How Would You Decide When Not To Use An Agentic Approach?

Why they might ask:
The job is agentic AI focused, but good judgement means avoiding unnecessary agency.

Detailed answer:

> I would avoid an agentic approach when the workflow is stable, rules are clear, risk is high, and a deterministic or simpler AI pattern can meet the requirement. Agents introduce flexibility, but also inconsistency, harder evaluation, tool-misuse risk and greater operational complexity.
>
> For example, if the task is to route a case based on fixed policy rules, deterministic logic may be safer. If the task is to answer from a known set of internal documents, RAG with structured output may be enough. If the task is classification on structured data, traditional ML may be more measurable and easier to govern.
>
> I would use an agent when the task genuinely requires multi-step investigation, dynamic tool selection, context gathering and reasoning over uncertain evidence. Even then, I would bound the agent with scoped tools, human review, evaluation and audit logs.
>
> The lead engineer judgement is to choose the simplest pattern that delivers value safely.

Follow-up pointers:

- Say "agentic" is not automatically better.
- Mention cost, latency, evaluation and governance.
- Link to Lloyds risk and customer trust.
