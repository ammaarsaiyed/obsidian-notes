## Likely Direct Questions And Short Answers

Q: What are your top three strengths for this role?

> Agentic AI hands-on experience, production engineering discipline, and Lloyds-specific regulated delivery context.

Q: What is your main development area?

> Formal lead-level leadership. I have led through technical direction, reusable patterns and stakeholder communication, and I want to keep growing in mentoring, architecture influence and broader team leadership.

Q: What does "art of the possible" mean to you?

> It means helping stakeholders see what AI could change, but grounding that in evidence, constraints and risk. The output should be a prioritised opportunity, not a vague demo.

Q: How do you move fast safely?

> Small experiments, clear hypotheses, low blast radius, early risk input, evals, logging and decision checkpoints.

Q: How do you decide whether to use GenAI?

> I ask whether the task needs language reasoning, summarisation, retrieval or flexible tool use. If deterministic logic or traditional ML is safer and sufficient, I would use that.

Q: What is your view on human-in-the-loop?

> Human-in-the-loop is essential where outputs affect customers, complaints, risk or regulated decisions. The system should support colleagues, surface evidence and uncertainty, and keep accountability clear.

Q: How do you explain AI risk to a non-technical stakeholder?

> "The system can be useful, but it can also be wrong in confident ways. We need to know when it is allowed to act, what evidence it uses, how humans review it, and how we detect problems."

Q: How do you handle multiple priorities?

> I prioritise by customer/business impact, risk, dependency, reversibility and learning value. For research work, I favour experiments that reduce the most uncertainty fastest.

Q: Tell me about a tricky technical problem.

> Use either LLM serving performance or context-window degradation. Emphasise diagnosis, trade-offs, measurement and operational impact.

Q: Tell me about stakeholder engagement.

> Use Lead Data Scientist presentations or Hive requirements analysis. Emphasise translating ambiguous needs into testable workflows and decisions.

Q: How do you contribute to AI literacy?

> I present technical work, write reusable modules/patterns, explain concepts in plain language, and help people understand risks as well as capabilities.

Q: How would you explain agents to a grandparent?

> A normal chatbot answers a question. An agent is more like an assistant that can decide which approved tools to use, look things up, take several steps, and then explain what it found. In a bank, we would keep that assistant carefully supervised.

Q: How would you explain embeddings?

> Embeddings turn text into numbers that capture meaning, so similar pieces of text end up close together. That lets systems search by meaning rather than only exact keywords.

Q: How would you explain model drift?

> Model drift is when the world changes after a model is built, so the patterns it learned no longer match reality as well.

Q: How would you explain explainability?

> It is being able to understand why a system gave an answer well enough to trust, challenge or correct it.
